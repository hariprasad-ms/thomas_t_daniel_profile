ABOUT ME

I am Thomas T. Daniel, and I received my Ph.D. in Nanotechnology from the Indian Institute of Technology (IIT) Guwahati, focusing on device fabrication for sensing applications. Along with my Ph.D. advisor (Prof. Roy P. Paily, IIT Guwahati), I started K-FAB Tech Private Limited, a startup firm for making semiconductor fabrication equipment easily affordable for researchers. My experiences include serving as Project Manager for cleanroom at the India Innovation Centre for Graphene (IICG) and providing technical consultancy for several academic and research institutions in establishing cutting-edge micro and nanofabrication facilities.

During my doctoral studies, I designed, modeled, fabricated, and characterized several devices to understand the electrical schemes they demonstrate. To fabricate these devices, I employed a wide range of materials including (i) semiconducting materials, (ii) magnetic materials, and (iii) magneto-semiconducting heterostructures. For this, I have been involving myself in the various stages of device fabrication, ranging from additive micro-fabrication techniques till their characterization. These experiences helped me to obtain a comprehensive understanding of electronic, optical, structural and sensing properties demonstrated by these materials and devices.

My research endeavors primarily revolve around the fabrication and characterization of gas sensors, particularly focusing on their electrical and magnetic properties. To achieve high-performance outcomes, I tactically modify the device's current-voltage characteristics by deploying interface engineering. My research also focuses on the thermionic emission phenomena in Schottky diodes, coupled with computational simulations, providing a valuable understanding of electron transport mechanisms, particularly in the context of semiconductor-metal interfaces.

As part of my research responsibilities, I was extensively involved in wet and dry oxidation processes, thin film deposition, as well as boron/phosphorus diffusion techniques. Wet and dry oxidation processes are essential for establishing crucial silicon dioxide (SiO$_2$) layers pivotal in device fabrication. I ensure the formation of high-quality SiO$_2$ layers vital for the functionality and reliability of microelectronic devices. Additionally, silicon diffusion processes serve a pivotal role in modulating the electrical properties of silicon, facilitating precise control over doping concentrations and carrier mobility within the silicon substrate. These techniques collectively contribute to advancing semiconductor device fabrication and enhancing their performance across various applications. Throughout my research, I explore a diverse array of additive manufacturing and patterning technologies to fabricate a range of devices, including Resistors, Schottky diodes, and Field-effect transistors, among others. To overcome the difficulties of fabricating the devices and performing sensing characterization, I developed the following systems.

  • Controlled environmental chamber for gas sensor calibration

    • The system has been designed to blend various gases, utilizing mass flow controllers, at different concentrations ranging from parts per billion (ppb) to parts per million (ppm).

  • Micro fabrication-based printing technology

    • A novel working prototype is demonstrated for printing emerging electronic components and novel devices, thus offering a potential alternative to traditional microfabrication-based technologies in terms of cost, rapid fabrication and simplicity.

These fabricated devices serve a crucial purpose in detecting various gases present in breath samples. Among the targeted gases are Nitric oxide (NO), Oxygen (O$_2$), Carbon dioxide (CO$_2$), Hydrogen (H$_2$), water vapor (H$_2$O), and traces of volatile organic compounds (VOCs). Importantly, the sensitivity of these sensors extends to levels ranging from parts per million (ppm) to sub-parts per million (sub-ppm), making them highly effective for detecting even trace amounts of gases. In essence, my doctoral research in sensor fabrication and characterization stands as a proof-of-concept for the continued development of healthcare technologies, providing possibilities for better patient care and diagnosis. Furthermore, my experience in microfabrication-based printing technology has enabled the rapid fabrication of electronic components and novel devices, offering a potential alternative to traditional microfabrication-based technologies in terms of cost, rapid fabrication, and simplicity.

Following my doctoral studies, I joined IICG as a project manager, overseeing cleanroom projects aimed at developing micro/nanoelectronics devices. My role involves selecting optimal cleanroom site, optimizing area utilization for different cleanroom classes, planning equipment placement and load distribution, determining exhaust requirements, and designing utilities with sizing and routing. Through strategic planning and precise execution, I ensure the seamless integration of facilities essential for advancing micro/nanoelectronics technologies. Collaborating with industry, academia, and startups, I focus on commercializing breakthrough electronic technologies. I took the initiative at IICG in developing a metal-semiconductor-metal photodetector with a response time of less than 15 $\mu$s. Additionally, I developed a device that can detect soil moisture in a range of 0\% to 50\% in a variety of soil compositions. My experience at IICG provides insights into industry research, while my consultancy work involves establishing cleanroom facilities for specialized production and research. As a Promoter and Technology Officer Remote of K-FAB Tech, I mentor employees to innovate in semiconductor device fabrication, leveraging my startup experience to bridge academia and industry.
